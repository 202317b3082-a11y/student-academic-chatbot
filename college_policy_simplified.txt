Policy: BITS (Birla Institute of Technology and Science) Pilani programs are taught both on campus and through off-campus Work Integrated Learning (WIL, where you work while studying) or in partnership with other organizations. These rules apply to both types unless stated otherwise. You need to follow your program structure, semester pattern, how academics work, when and how to register, how your work is evaluated, and what you need to graduate. Every academic year has two main semesters called the first semester and second semester.

A first degree student can normally register for up to 25 units, while a student doing a higher degree can normally register for up to 20 units, but this does not include deficiency courses or audit courses (courses you take for knowledge but they do not count toward your degree). Some programs may have different unit limits. The summer term usually runs for about eight weeks and moves faster than regular semesters.

A student can normally register for at most three courses with a maximum of 10 units during summer, depending on what courses are available. Certain courses like Practice School (PS, which is your internship at a company), dissertation (long research paper for advanced degrees), thesis (long research paper for undergraduate degrees), and similar intensive courses may not be offered during summer. How long your program takes depends on what the Institute Bulletin (main rule book) says, but to get your degree you must complete all the courses and units your program requires.

Minimum unit requirements are different for different degrees. Bachelor of Science (B.Sc.) off-campus programs need 105 units, combined four-year undergraduate degrees need 144 units, Master of Engineering (M.E.) or Master of Pharmacy (M.Pharm.) need 64 units, and other programs have their own specific unit requirements. One academic unit means you do about three hours of work per week, including classroom time, lab work, and self-study.

The semester-wise program shows the normal amount of courses you should take each semester for regular progress. This normal load is mainly for planning and does not automatically give you any special rights or exceptions. You should follow the semester-by-semester course plan given in the Bulletin because courses are connected to each other and build on each other. Some courses have prerequisites (you must complete another course first), while some groups of courses need you to have done prior preparation.

Prescribed program means all the required courses across all your semesters, and prescribed semester courses are the courses you are supposed to take in a particular semester. The way academics is managed is done by authorized groups like the Academic Governing Committee (AGC, the group that makes academic decisions), Departmental Committee on Academics (DCA, your department's academic committee), Doctoral Counselling Committee (DCC, for PhD students), and Departmental Research Committee (DRC, for research matters). These committees decide about admissions, course plans, prerequisites, moving between programs, changes to programs, PhD matters, and other academic choices. These decisions must follow rules approved by the Senate (the main decision-making group).

If you do not follow your required course order, you may build up a backlog (unpassed courses from earlier semesters). Backlog must be fixed quickly because the longer you wait, the longer your total program will take and the worse your academic standing becomes. If your backlog is large or your grades are poor, the Academic Counselling Board (ACB, a group that helps struggling students) will put you under controlled registration and monitoring (they will decide which courses you can take and check your progress).

You must register at the start of every semester. You cannot attend classes or use the Institute buildings without properly registering. Registration is not just a formality; it confirms which courses you are taking and must follow academic rules, fee rules, timetable rules, prerequisites, and approval from the Dean (head of your academic section). At the end of each semester, your performance is recorded using letter grades (like A, B, C), non-letter grades, or reports. Grade sheets show your course results and Cumulative Grade Point Average (CGPA, which is your overall grade).

A transcript is a record of all your grade sheets over time with explanations. Your CGPA shows your overall academic performance and is used to determine divisions (like First Class, Second Class) after you graduate, where applicable. A course is considered cleared (passed) when you get a valid grade. If you repeat a course or take it again, your latest grade decides whether it counts as passed and affects your CGPA.

Reports like W (Withdrawn, you dropped the course), RC (Registration Cancelled, your registration was cancelled), RRA (Required to Register Again, you must retake it), NC (Not Cleared, you did not pass), I (Incomplete, you did not finish), or GA (Grade Awaited, your grade is being processed) have specific meanings and must be understood according to the academic rules. The rules do not set a required attendance percentage for taking tests or exams, but you are expected to attend classes and labs regularly, take all scheduled tests and exams, and complete assigned work.

If you do not cooperate enough for your instructor to evaluate your work, they may give you an NC (Not Cleared grade). A registered student, a student who withdrew, or a student allowed to stay away remains enrolled in the Institute for that semester. If you cannot register for a good reason, you must get prior permission from your Dean. If you fail to register without permission, your name can be removed from the rolls (official records).

Using academic flexibilities (special allowances) does not automatically mean you get money back. Refunds follow the fee schedule that is currently in use. Some flexibilities may require you to pay extra fees. You must pay any money you owe and pay your semester fees in advance to avoid being blocked from registering or having your grade sheets held back.

BITS education has three levels: first level is four-year undergraduate degrees, second level is Master's degrees (higher degrees), and third level is PhD programs. Each level has its own course structure, units, elective choices, special courses, academic requirements, how you are monitored, and graduation requirements, but all follow the same basic academic principles.

Program courses are divided into types: compulsory courses (you must take these), discipline core courses (main courses in your subject), discipline electives (optional courses in your subject), humanities electives (optional general education courses), or open electives (optional courses from anywhere). Named compulsory courses are specifically listed in your semester plan. Electives are chosen from a list of approved courses. Open electives let you study subjects outside your main subject and general education requirements, as long as you meet the course requirements. First degree students must complete the required electives in each category.

You may optionally take up to four extra electives, depending on limits and approvals. A first degree student with a required Cumulative Grade Point Average (CGPA) may be allowed to take one higher degree course per semester, usually counted as an open elective unless it is listed as a discipline elective. After finishing your required first degree courses, you may be allowed to register for at most one more semester with approvals.

This may be for extra open electives or to complete a minor (second subject area). You must register for at least three new courses with at least nine units and take them seriously. Practice School (PS) and Thesis are intensive academic projects. Practice School must be done full time with no other courses. A 16-unit thesis must also be full time, but a 9-unit thesis may allow you to take up to three other courses at the same time, totaling at most nine units, depending on regulations and approvals.

Higher degree programs (Master's degrees) include core courses, electives, technical communication, research practice, dissertation (research paper), internship (work at a company), or Practice School depending on your program. You normally register for Practice School or internship only after completing your other required courses. Dissertation usually comes after coursework, but the Departmental Committee on Academics (DCA) may allow special arrangements in unusual cases. The PhD program develops broad knowledge before you start research.

PhD students may need coursework, qualifying examination (a test you must pass), language requirement, teaching practice or research methodology, seminar (group study), independent study, and thesis. PhD research must meet high scholarly standards and may involve campus research, industry research, collaborative research, sponsored research, or approved outside research settings.

You cannot register if you owe the Institute money, your grade sheet is being held, you have an Incomplete report (unfinished work) from a previous semester, or you have been told to stay away. Grade Awaited (GA, meaning your grade is delayed) cases are handled by your Dean according to specific rules. Late registration may be allowed only in unusual situations at the Dean's discretion. The Dean will decide if you have to pay a fine. If you register more than one week after classes start, you need the Dean's approval, especially for off-campus courses, Practice School, thesis, dissertation, and special courses.

The first approved course list is your original registration. Changes you request are amended registration, usually through course substitution or withdrawal. Changes made by the Institute due to rules, discipline, cancellation, PhD entry, or transfer are revised registration. All changes must follow deadlines, priorities, and Dean-approved academic conditions.

Before registering for any course, you must meet the prerequisite conditions (earlier courses). A prerequisite connects two courses: you must pass the earlier course with a required grade before taking the later course. Registering for a course when you have not met prerequisites can get your registration cancelled and your grade rejected. Prior preparation means you must pass a set of earlier courses before registering for special courses or main course groups.

Practice School (PS) I normally needs you to have completed earlier semesters. Practice School II or thesis needs you to have passed all required named courses, including PS I where applicable. Your Department's Committee on Academics (DCA) may recommend modified patterns in limited cases where you have small gaps in preparation. You cannot register for a course if you have already taken more advanced courses in that area or if you are not prepared enough for the content, unless rules allow it.

Your semester schedule should have no timetable conflicts. If a required course is no longer offered, your Dean may assign an academically similar current course with the same unit value to meet that requirement. Backlog includes courses from earlier semesters that you did not clear, courses with unresolved NC (Not Cleared) grades, and courses where your latest grade is not high enough for the next course in the sequence. Current semester courses are PSC (Prescribed Semester Courses), while later courses are higher level courses.

A student with backlog must prioritize registering for backlog first before taking PSC or higher level courses. Backlog is the first priority for registration. You must register for all operative backlog (backlog that you still need to complete) before prescribed semester courses, and you can take higher level or repeat courses only after handling backlog and prescribed semester courses.

Operational backlog and operational prescribed semester courses account for available courses, prerequisites, timetable conflicts, and Dean decisions. You may repeat a cleared (passed) course for grade improvement only if the Institute allows it and the course is in your current program. You cannot repeat courses after you have completed all graduation requirements or when only restricted courses remain.

Practice School, thesis, seminar, internship, dissertation, project courses, and barred courses usually cannot be repeated on your own choice. You may take a higher level course only after registering for all operative backlog and all prescribed semester courses. Higher level courses cannot be used to skip backlog or prescribed courses. Any later changes must remove higher level and repeat courses first before changing current semester or backlog courses.

You may start some electives earlier or later than the normal semester plan, at your own responsibility. This flexibility helps you plan your elective requirements across your program. However, this is only for registration management and does not remove prerequisites, prior preparation, CGPA requirements, minimum academic standards, graduation rules, or course availability limits.

Within two weeks of semester start, or one week in summer, you may request to replace one registered course. You can normally withdraw from a course within ten weeks of semester start, or five weeks in summer. Exceptional withdrawal may be allowed for genuine serious circumstances by your Dean.

Official authorities may change or cancel your registration if it violates rules, if you are found doing unfair practices or breaking discipline rules, if you deliberately do not pay dues, if your program status changes, or if you leave the Institute. Reports may include RC (Registration Cancelled), RRA (Required to Register Again), or DP (Discontinued from Programme) depending on the academic result.

Teaching aims to develop curiosity, logical thinking, independent learning, and ability to solve new problems. Your instructors organize course delivery, evaluation methods, materials, assignments, class discussions, and learning support. You must participate by doing self-study, attending classes, taking assessments, doing lab work, completing assignments, and actively participating in academics.

Within one week of classes starting, your instructor must share the course handout (outline). It must explain the course pace, what topics are covered, textbooks, assignments, how you will be graded, quizzes, tests, exams, attendance expectations, schedules, grade weightage, make-up exam policy, mid-semester grading, final grading method, and other course details.

Structured courses must have at least three grading components including a comprehensive exam at the end of the semester. Grading should be spread across the semester. First degree courses need at least 20 percent open-book written tests or take-home assignments, while higher degree courses need at least 40 percent open-book grading components. You must attend all evaluations and turn in work on time.

If you miss a quiz, test, exam, or assignment, you must immediately tell your instructor. If you have a good reason, the instructor may arrange a make-up exam. The instructor's decision on make-up exams is final. Your instructors should give feedback regularly. Exam papers should be graded quickly, shown to you for clarification, and returned when possible. Instructors should discuss test results with the class, including the highest, lowest, and average scores, so you understand what you need to improve.

Letter grades have point values: A (Excellent) is 10 points, A- (Very Good) is 9 points, B (Good) is 8 points, B- (Above Average) is 7 points, C (Fair or Average) is 6 points, C- (Below Average) is 5 points, D (Poor) is 4 points, and E (Exposed/Failed) is 2 points. Some courses use non-letter grades instead of points.

Seminar (group study), independent study, teaching practice, internship, dissertation, project work, design project, capstone project, audit courses, and PhD thesis may receive descriptive grades like Excellent, Good, Fair, Poor, Satisfactory, Unsatisfactory, Acceptable, or Unacceptable depending on the course type. Academic reports like I (Incomplete), GA (Grade Awaited), W (Withdrawn), RC (Registration Cancelled), RRA (Required to Register Again), DP (Discontinued from Programme), NC (Not Cleared), TGA (Thesis Grade Awaited), and AC (Allowed to Continue) are not grades but record academic situations.

I (Incomplete) means you have pending requirements. GA (Grade Awaited) means your grade is being processed. W (Withdrawn) is recorded when you approve course withdrawal and is ignored for operations that look at performance. RC (Registration Cancelled) means your registration was cancelled. If you must retake the course, it is reported as RRA (Required to Register Again). If cancellation means you must leave your program, it is reported as DP (Discontinued from Programme).

NC (Not Cleared) is given when a registered student does not provide enough opportunity for evaluation by missing components, appearing only formally (just to be counted), submitting blank work, or not cooperating with academics. In required courses, NC means you must retake the course for a valid grade. In electives, you may repeat the course or choose a different elective depending on requirements.

Your Cumulative Grade Point Average (CGPA) is the weighted average of all your letter grades from start to latest semester. Your course units act as weights. If you retake a course and get a new grade, the new grade replaces the old grade in CGPA calculation. Reports alone do not change CGPA. Grade sheets may be held if you owe money, have an unfair means case pending, or have a discipline case pending.

Your instructors must keep records of attendance, evaluation scores, class averages, mid-semester grades, exam papers, and course outlines for at least two semesters after the course ends. These records support review and academic monitoring.

At the end of each semester, first degree students should not have more than one E (failed) grade and must keep a CGPA of at least 4. Higher degree students should not have any E grades and must keep a CGPA of at least 5. You must also show progress in completing required courses.

Not meeting minimum academic requirements is called affliction and puts you under Academic Counselling Board (ACB) supervision. While under ACB, you lose normal registration choices like picking courses, choosing electives, repeating courses, taking higher level courses, replacing courses, and withdrawing from courses. ACB decides which courses you take and monitors your progress.

ACB estimates what you are missing, sets semester goals, may require summer registration, and checks your progress. If you do not meet goals or do not respond, ACB can require you to leave the Institute. It may also recommend moving to an easier program if available. ACB decisions are final.

Practice School (PS) connects classroom learning with real professional work. Your PS evaluation follows continuous monitoring and looks at intellectual ability, teamwork, leadership, initiative, professional judgment, responsibility, problem solving, punctuality, meeting deadlines, speaking skills, and writing skills. You may get a Practice School Transcript showing your professional personality assessment.

Practice School I (PS I) normally needs preparation from earlier semesters. Practice School II (PS II) needs you to have completed all required named courses including PS I. You cannot take any other course while doing Practice School. Your Dean for Practice School (Dean PS) decides when you do PS, where you are placed, delays, early completion, and related matters. The Dean's decision is final.

You may move from Practice School option to thesis or dissertation option within allowed times and with Dean PS permission. Ignoring your PS placement may mean you lose the PS option. Bad progress or unprofessional conduct in PS can lead to discontinuation from the PS option, with possible move to thesis or dissertation route.

Thesis or dissertation registration needs prior academic preparation. First degree thesis normally needs all required named courses completed. Higher degree dissertation usually comes after coursework. Your topic and supervisor are assigned by DCA (Department Committee on Academics) with Dean approval. Your final thesis or dissertation must be a coherent English document with supervisor approval and submitted within the set time.

A first degree student may choose a 16-unit thesis with no other courses, or a 9-unit thesis that may need extra electives to meet graduation requirements. A 9-unit thesis student may register for at most three courses at the same time totaling at most nine units, normally counted as open electives. If thesis or dissertation is not submitted in time, your last registration may be cancelled and RRA reported.

Evaluation is done by appointed examiners including the supervisor, followed by viva (oral exam) where applicable. NC (Not Cleared) in thesis, seminar, or dissertation means you must register again for the same number of units or as decided by authority.

BITS provides academic flexibilities like admissions in both semesters, advanced standing (entering with credit from previous study), marginal deficiency (starting with one course gap), transfer (changing programs), dual degree (two degrees at once), minor programs (second subject area), thesis or Practice School options, electives, course repetition, withdrawal, substitution, audit, summer term, and pace variation. These flexibilities are conditional, competitive where applicable, and must not reduce academic standards.

Admissions may happen in both semesters depending on seats and facilities. In first tier (undergraduate) programs, most admissions are in the first semester. Second semester admission may happen for a small number of equally competitive students due to program flexibility, but this is not guaranteed.

Summer course offerings are voluntary and limited. During summer, there is no prescribed semester course concept, but backlog remains the first priority. You may register for at most three courses if rules allow. Higher level summer courses are allowed only when you have completed backlog conditions and prerequisite requirements.

Dual degree is a competitive opportunity for capable students pursuing two combined four-year degrees. The single degree semester plan is replaced by a combined program. Requirements include general Institute courses, subject requirements of both degrees, electives, and Practice School or thesis. The two degrees are completed together, not separately. A dual degree student cannot drop one degree and continue with the other.

Leaving dual degree needs written request and approval. Deliberately leaving the approved dual degree program may cancel the dual degree facility and move you back to the first degree under current rules.

Transfer means moving from one program, stream, scheme, or level to another before finishing your first one. When transferring, your full academic record is checked. Completed courses may be counted in the new program where suitable, remaining courses are handled by rules, and your CGPA plus any academic penalties continue with you.

A student with preparation higher than the minimum entrance requirement may be admitted with advanced standing (getting credit for previous study). Previous academic record is compared with program requirements, and tests may be used if needed. Exemption is usually only for required courses, while electives in the new program generally must be completed at BITS.

A strong student with small gaps in preparation may be admitted with marginal deficiency. They may need to complete extra courses beyond the normal program. Such students enter with a form of backlog, and CGPA or division is calculated using both required program courses and the additional courses they needed.

Audit courses are mainly for learning, especially for students not pursuing a degree. Completing an audit course does not count as passing a course for degree requirements and cannot automatically become a credit course later. Practice School, thesis, dissertation, collaborative program courses, and other restricted courses are normally not available on audit.

Minor programs let first degree students focus on an area outside their main subject. You may pursue at most one minor and should normally declare it by the end of second year. To complete a minor you need required core and elective courses, minimum units, minimum CGPA of 4.50 in minor courses, and degree completion.

PhD admission is initially provisional and becomes formal after passing the qualifying examination. The Departmental Research Committee (DRC) recommends a supervisor or mentor and appoints a Doctoral Advisory Committee (DAC). You must complete coursework, qualifying exam, language requirement, teaching practice or research methodology, seminar, thesis work, and other required components on time.

PhD students with a first degree background may take at least six courses with at least 24 units. You must not get below D grade in any course and must keep minimum CGPA 5. Students with higher degree background may have zero to six courses depending on research area and preparation.

Before registering for thesis, a PhD student must pass the qualifying examination within the set time. The exam normally has written and oral parts and tests basic knowledge and ability to handle new situations. A student who fails or misses it may get only one more attempt at DRC discretion.

A PhD student may need to show ability to translate material from a modern European language into English, unless waived. This requirement may be fulfilled through prior courses, audit courses, or special test. Teaching Practice or Practice Lecture Series and Research Methodology help train teaching, communication, research analysis, and data skills.

After passing the qualifying exam, the PhD student selects a topic, supervisor, and co-supervisor if needed, prepares a research proposal, and presents it to the Doctoral Advisory Committee (DAC). DRC evaluates whether the topic fits research goals, facilities are available, supervisor is suitable, and workload is manageable. The Dean for Academic Research (Dean AGSR) approves the proposal.

Thesis registration is allowed only after topic and supervisor approval. Final PhD thesis should normally be submitted within ten semesters after passing the qualifying examination. Maximum duration including extra time and semester withdrawals is generally fourteen semesters, with special provisions for maternity leave and unusual cases.

Submission needs thesis copies, soft copy, summary, supervisor approval of original work, Doctoral Advisory Committee (DAC) reports, examiner list, and fee receipt. PhD thesis is examined by two Vice-Chancellor-appointed examiners. Decisions may approve viva (oral exam), request small or major corrections, require revision and resubmission, or reject the thesis.

Disagreements may involve discussion or a third examiner. If approved, viva-voce is held, possibly online in unusual situations, and final thesis grade is given. A PhD student may not continue if the qualifying exam is not passed in time, CGPA stays below 5.50 beyond allowed recovery, two consecutive thesis interim grades are unsatisfactory, thesis or revised thesis is not submitted in time, thesis is rejected, final verdict is not obtained, or final thesis grade is unacceptable.

A first degree or higher degree student graduates after clearing all required courses, completing thesis, seminar, dissertation, or unit requirements where applicable, meeting minimum CGPA of 4.50 for first degree or B.Sc. and 5.50 for higher degree, staying outside Academic Counselling Board (ACB) control, resolving NC consequences, and satisfying all academic rules.

A student becomes eligible for the degree only after satisfying graduation requirements, evaluation rules, and having no pending discipline or unfair means cases. If the student has outstanding dues to the Institute, hostel, or recognized student organization, the degree may be held until those dues are fully paid.

Integrated four-year degrees and Bachelor of Science (B.Sc.) working professional programs use CGPA-based division classification. Distinction needs CGPA 9.00 or more. First Division needs CGPA 7.00 or more but less than 9. Second Division needs CGPA 4.50 or more but less than 7. Diploma, higher degree, and PhD programs do not get divisions.

Off-campus Work Integrated Learning (WILP) and Collaborative programs are run by the WILP Division. Dean WILP is responsible for registration, evaluation, monitoring, and program operations. An employed person admitted to an off-campus program is treated as both a full-time student and a full-time employee within the work-integrated model.

Off-campus programs need a viable learning environment, employer support, facilities, motivation, and mentors or tutors. You may need organization (company) approval and mentor details during admission. The mentor guides self-study and connects you with instructors. If sponsorship, work environment, or collaborating organization support becomes unavailable, you or the program may be discontinued.

Moving between on-campus and off-campus programs is not automatic and is treated as new admission, not ordinary transfer. Off-campus to on-campus movement needs competitive admission method and essential requirements. Off-campus dual degree is generally not valid because programs target specific populations, though rare exceptions may be reviewed by the Academic Governing Committee (AGC).

WILP and collaborative programs may have semester-wise course structures, but they are less rigid than on-campus structures. You may be permitted to take fewer courses or have gaps, and pace is calculated accordingly. Work experience, internship, dissertation, project work, capstone project, Practice School, and similar courses are normally taken after other coursework.

Off-campus students must follow both BITS academic rules and discipline or code of conduct applicable in collaborating organizations. Failure to follow may result in discontinuation from the program. If organizational facilities become unavailable, the Institute may try to solve it, but you must cooperate and the Institute may have limited responsibility.

Off-campus WILP students must not get any E (failed) grade, must keep CGPA at least 4.50 for first degree or first level diploma and 5.50 for higher degree or higher level diploma, and must show required progress. Failure puts the student under Academic Monitoring Board (similar to ACB).

WILP students are responsible for completing all evaluation components according to each course outline. Evaluation includes quizzes and assignments as EC-1 (Evaluation Component 1), Mid-Semester Test as EC-2 (Evaluation Component 2), and Comprehensive Examination as EC-3 (Evaluation Component 3). You must track schedules and attend required tests and exams for every course you take in the current semester.

For all courses registered in a WILP semester, you must attend the Mid-Semester Test and Comprehensive Examination. Missing these required components without approved make-up can seriously affect course completion. Do not assume assignments alone are enough because EC-2 and EC-3 are required evaluation components.

If you cannot appear in regular mid-semester or comprehensive examination due to genuine unavoidable personal or professional reasons, you may apply online for make-up tests or exams soon after regular exams are over. Make-up is for missed exams, not for performance improvement or second attempt.

If you have a backlog course with two exams on the same date and time, you must appear for the regular test or exam of one clashing course and take the make-up test or exam for the other. This avoids double absence while respecting schedule conflict.

If you are absent for both regular and make-up mid-semester or comprehensive exam in a course, you must register again for that course in a later semester and complete all evaluation components again. Marks from any evaluation component of the earlier semester cannot be carried forward to the later semester.

You must not take both the regular and corresponding make-up exam for the same EC-2 or EC-3 component in a course. Make-up exams cannot be used to improve marks. If you appear for both, your course registration may be cancelled and you must register again later.

Students with E grade (failed) or RRA (Required to Register Again) in any semester course must register again for the same course later. For every semester registration, you must pay required semester fees regardless of number of courses. There is no separate per-course exam fee, and paid semester fees cannot be carried forward to a later semester.

BITS Pilani has zero tolerance for unfair means or malpractice during examinations. Students found involved in such activities face discipline action under examination committee rules. Consequences may be severe and can include cancellation, registration consequences, academic penalties, or even discontinuation from the program depending on seriousness.

Bachelor of Science (B.Sc.) Design and Computing is an eight-semester program for young IT professionals with 10+2 (12th grade) qualification. It combines computing, computers, mathematics, design, and general education. The program builds ability to think through and create computing-driven solutions for different customer needs using technical knowledge and design thinking.

The B.Sc. program allows learning without stopping your career through online classes mostly on weekends or after business hours. It includes hardware, software, storage, networking, infrastructure management, simulation software, deployment environments, CloudSim (simulation tool), NS2 (network simulator), Net-SNMP (network tool), CPU-OS Simulator, Amazon compute and storage platforms, Apache cloud suite, and open-source tools.

The program uses continuous evaluation at regular intervals to provide quick feedback and keep working professionals on track. Delivery mixes classroom and experiential learning through labs, assignments, case studies, simulations, group discussions, and work-integrated activities. Successful graduates join the BITS Pilani alumni (graduate) community.

The program prepares IT professionals to apply design thinking to computing-driven solutions. Learning outcomes include understanding hardware and software, solving problems using programming and software, applying discrete mathematics and statistics, understanding general education perspectives, understanding design principles, and applying design thinking in software contexts.

The Bachelor of Science Design and Computing curriculum includes 24 core courses and 4 electives. Core courses include 10 in computers and computing, 4 in mathematics, 5 in design, and 5 in general education. Electives come from a pool shared with the employer organization and are chosen based on preferences, business needs, feasibility, and BITS discretion.

The program is spread across eight semesters with courses such as Writing Practice, Symbolic Logic, Electrical Sciences, Probability and Statistics, Digital Design, Data Structures, Linear Algebra, Creative Thinking, Computer Programming, Discrete Structures, Environmental Studies, Object Oriented Programming, Computer Organization, Cultural Studies, Software Design, Statistical Inference, HCI (Human Computer Interaction), Information Security, electives, Design Project, and Capstone Project.

Classroom sessions are conducted by BITS Pilani instructors over weekends, usually 1.5 to 2 hours per subject. Since you typically take four courses, you may attend around four classroom sessions over a weekend across about sixteen weekends per semester. Live online classes are accessible from any location with a computer and high-speed internet.

In the final semester, participants complete a semester-long intensive project work using concepts learned during the program, guided by organization mentor and supervisor. You may access virtual labs and instructor expertise. Digital learning material such as lecture videos, student notes, and curated content is available through a mobile-friendly learning management platform.

Learner performance is assessed continuously through quizzes, assignments, mid-semester exams, and comprehensive exams. Each course has at least one assignment or quiz, a mid-semester exam, and a final comprehensive exam. Exams are usually 2 to 3 hours, often on weekends, and locations are communicated at semester start.

Eligible applicants are trainees or employed professionals who completed 10+2 (12th grade) in Science with Mathematics and Physics or equivalent qualification, with minimum 60 percent total score, minimum 60 percent each in Mathematics and Physics, and at least one year relevant work experience within your organization.

Business Mathematics is not treated as equal to Mathematics. Meeting minimum eligibility does not guarantee admission. BITS Pilani makes the final admission decision after overall review of your application information.

The Admission Cell evaluates documents, eligibility, completeness, and accuracy. Selected candidates are usually informed by email within two weeks of submitting the complete application and supporting documents. For new admission information, the fee schedule includes application fee, admission fee, and semester fee.

The fee information mentions INR (Indian Rupees) 500 application fee, INR 5,500 admission fee, and INR 29,500 semester fee. You should always verify the latest fee schedule from the official admission portal before payment.

Applicants create login at the Online Application Center using official company email ID, fill and submit the application form, pay the application fee, and upload required documents such as academic certificates, transcripts, photograph, and supporting files. Acceptable upload formats include DOC, DOCX, PDF, ZIP, and JPEG.

BITS Pilani WILP (Work Integrated Learning Program) programs are rigorous higher education programs for working professionals and are designed around work integration. Their philosophy and teaching are different from open or distance learning. The purpose is to deliver education in workplace-connected contexts with quality curriculum, teaching, skill development, knowledge improvement, and productivity improvement.

Eligibility Sheet tracks required courses, performance, grades, reports, repeats, registration outcomes, backlog, and graduation progress. For repeated courses, the latest non-ignored performance decides action. W (Withdrawn) and clear RC (Registration Cancelled) are ignored by looking backward.

Eligibility Sheet helps with registration planning, CGPA updates, minimum requirement checks, and graduation verification. Before registration, authorities check whether the student can register, identify backlog and prescribed semester courses, ensure backlog first priority, verify prerequisites, prior preparation, being too prepared or not prepared enough, elective conditions, audit limits, and timetable fit.

Students usually cannot take other courses with Practice School, Thesis-Seminar, Internship, or similar exclusive components.
