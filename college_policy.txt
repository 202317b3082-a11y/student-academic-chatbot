

Policy: BITS Pilani programmes run on campus and through off-campus Work Integrated Learning or collaborative formats. These regulations apply to both unless a clause says otherwise. Students should follow the programme structure, semester pattern, academic procedures, registration rules, evaluation rules, and graduation requirements. Every academic year has two regular semesters called first semester and second semester.

A first degree student may normally register up to 25 units, while a higher degree student may normally register up to 20 units, excluding permitted deficiency or audit courses. Some programmes may have special unit limits. Summer term normally lasts about eight weeks and runs at an accelerated pace.

A student can usually register for. at most three courses and not more than 10 units, subject to availability. Certain courses like Practice School, dissertation, thesis, and similar intensive courses may not be offered as ordinary summer courses. Programme duration is guided by the Institute Bulletin, but degree eligibility depends on completing prescribed courses and units.

Minimum unit requirements differ by. degree. B.Sc. off-campus programmes require 105 units, integrated first degrees require 144 units, M.E. or M.Pharm. require 64 units, and other programmes follow their specified unit requirements. One academic unit represents approximately three hours per week of total student effort, including class work, laboratory work, and self-study.

The semester-wise programme gives a normal load for. expected progress. Normal load is mainly a planning reference and does not by itself create extra rights or exceptions. Students should follow the semester-wise sequence given in the Bulletin because courses are academically connected. Some courses require prerequisites, while some groups of courses need prior preparation.

Prescribed programme means the full required course package across. semesters, and prescribed semester courses are the courses expected in a particular semester. Academic policies and operational details are handled by authorised bodies such as the Academic Governing Committee, Departmental Committee on Academics, Doctoral Counselling Committee, and Departmental Research Committee.

These committees guide admissions, curriculum, prerequisites, transfers, programme deviations, Ph.D. matters, and other academic decisions. within Senate-approved regulations. If a student does not follow the prescribed course sequence, backlog may accumulate. Backlog correction must be done quickly because delay can increase total programme duration and damage academic progression.

If backlog becomes large or academic performance becomes weak, the student may come under the Academic Counselling Board for controlled registration and. monitoring. Registration is compulsory at the beginning of every semester. A student cannot attend classes or use Institute facilities without completing proper registration.

Registration is not only a formality; it confirms the approved set of courses for the semester and must follow academic rules, fee rules, timetable constraints, prerequisites, and Dean approvals. At the end. of every semester, performance is recorded through letter grades, non-letter grades, or reports. Grade sheets show course outcomes and CGPA.

A transcript is a chronological record of grade sheets with explanations. CGPA reflects overall performance and is also used for division classification after programme completion where applicable. A course is considered cleared when the student. obtains a valid grade. If a course is repeated or registered again, the latest performance controls the clearing status and CGPA where applicable.

Reports such as W, RC, RRA, NC, I, or GA have specific consequences and must be interpreted according to the academic regulations. The regulations do not prescribe a minimum attendance percentage for. appearing in tests or exams, but students are expected to attend classes and laboratories regularly, appear for scheduled evaluations, and complete assigned work.

If a student does not cooperate enough for evaluation, the instructor may award an NC, meaning Not Cleared. A registered student, withdrawn student, or student permitted to stay away remains on Institute. rolls for that semester. If a student cannot register for valid reasons, prior permission must be taken from the concerned Dean.

Without permission, failure to register can lead to the student’s name being struck off the rolls. Applying academic regulations or using flexibilities does not automatically create any right to fee refund. Refunds are governed. only by the fee schedule in force. Some flexibilities may require additional fees.

Students must clear dues and pay required semester fees in advance to avoid registration blocks or withheld grade sheets. BITS education is arranged in three tiers: first tier integrated first degrees, second tier higher degrees, and third tier Ph.D. programmes. Each tier. has its own course structure, units, electives, special courses, academic requirements, monitoring methods, and graduation conditions while still following common academic principles.

Programme courses may be named compulsory courses, discipline core courses, discipline electives, humanities electives, or open electives. Named courses are specifically listed in the semester pattern. Electives are selected from approved course pools. Open electives let students study outside discipline and humanities requirements, subject to prerequisites and programme rules. First degree students must complete prescribed electives in each category.

They may optionally take up to four extra electives, subject to limits and approvals. A first degree student with a required CGPA may be allowed to take one higher. degree course per semester, usually counted as open elective unless listed as discipline elective. After fulfilling first degree graduation requirements, a student may be permitted to register for at most one additional semester with required approvals.

This may be allowed for extra open electives or minor completion. The student must register for at least three. new courses of at least nine units and pursue them seriously. Practice School and Thesis are intensive academic components. Practice School must be pursued full time with no other courses.

A 16-unit thesis must also be full time, while a 9-unit thesis may allow at most three concurrent courses totaling at most nine units, subject. to applicable regulations and approvals. Higher degree programmes include core courses, electives, technical communication, research practice, dissertation, internship, or Practice School depending on programme design.

Practice School or internship registration normally happens only after completing other required courses. Dissertation usually follows coursework, though DCA may permit special concurrent or split arrangements in extraordinary cases. The. Ph.D. programme develops broad competence before research.

Students may need coursework, qualifying examination, language requirement, teaching practice or research methodology, seminar or independent study, and thesis. Ph.D. research must meet scholarly standards and may involve campus, industry, collaborative, sponsored, or approved outside research settings.

A student cannot register if Institute dues are unpaid, grade sheet. is withheld, an Incomplete report from the previous semester exists, or the student has been debarred or asked to stay away. Grade Awaited cases are handled by the concerned Dean according to the specific rules for GA reports. Late registration may be allowed only in unusual circumstances at the discretion of the concerned Dean.

The. Dean may decide whether a fine applies. If late registration is considered beyond one week after classwork begins, concurrence of the concerned Dean must be obtained, especially for off-campus, Practice School, thesis, dissertation, and special courses. The first approved course list is original registration. A student-initiated change is amended registration, usually through course substitution or. withdrawal.

A change caused by official authority, discipline, cancellation, Ph.D. entry, transfer, or other events is revised registration. All changes must follow deadlines, priorities, and Dean-approved academic conditions. Before registering in any course, a student must satisfy its prerequisite conditions. A prerequisite usually connects two courses, where the earlier course must be cleared with the. required adequate grade before the later course can be taken.

Registration in violation of prerequisites can be cancelled, and any obtained grade may be rejected. Prior preparation means a required set of earlier courses must be cleared before registration in special courses or core course groups. PS I normally needs prior semesters cleared. PS II. or thesis requires all named courses, including PS I where applicable.

DCA may recommend modified patterns in limited cases with small unresolved prior preparation gaps. Students cannot register in a course if they are academically overprepared or underprepared for its content unless rules permit. Semester registration should be free from timetable conflicts.

If a mandatory. course is no longer offered, the concerned Dean may name an academically similar current course with the same unit value for that requirement. Backlog includes past-region courses not cleared, courses with unresolved NC, and courses whose latest grade is inadequate for a prerequisite chain. Current semester courses are PSC, while later courses are higher level. courses.

A student with backlog must prioritise backlog registration before moving forward to PSC or higher level courses. Backlog is the first charge on registration. A student must register in the full operative backlog before prescribed semester courses and can take higher level or repeat courses only after backlog and prescribed semester courses are handled.

Operational backlog and operational prescribed semester courses account for availability, prerequisites, timetable clashes, and Dean decisions. A student may repeat a cleared course for grade improvement only if facilities permit and the course is part of the current prescribed programme. Repetition is not allowed after graduation requirements are completed or when only exclusive courses remain.

Practice School, thesis, seminar, internship, dissertation, project courses, and barred courses usually cannot be repeated voluntarily. A student may take a higher level course only after registering for the full operative backlog and full prescribed semester courses. Higher level courses cannot be used to avoid backlog or prescribed courses.

Any later amendment must remove higher. level and repeat courses first before altering current semester or backlog courses. Students may advance or delay electives relative to the semester pattern at their own responsibility. This flexibility helps plan elective requirements across the programme.

However, it is only for registration management and does not remove prerequisites, prior preparation, CGPA requirements, minimum academic standards,. graduation rules, or restrictions on course availability. Within two weeks of semester start, or one week in summer term, a student may request substitution of a registered course.

Withdrawal from a course is normally allowed within ten weeks of semester start, or five weeks in summer term. Exceptional withdrawal may be permitted for genuine extenuating. circumstances by the concerned Dean.

Official authorities may revise or cancel registration if it violates rules, if the student is found guilty of unfair means or indiscipline, if dues are deliberately unpaid, if programme status changes, or if the student leaves Institute rolls. Reports may include RC, RRA, or DP depending on the academic consequence.

Teaching aims to develop curiosity, rational thinking, independent learning, and ability to handle unfamiliar problems. Instructors organize course delivery, evaluation, materials, assignments, class interaction, and learning support. Students must cooperate through self-study, attendance, assessments, laboratory work, assignments, and active participation in the academic process. Within one week of classwork, the instructor must announce the course. handout.

It should explain pace, syllabus coverage, textbooks, assignments, evaluation components, quizzes, tests, exams, attendance expectations, schedules, weightage, make-up policy, mid-semester grading, final grading method, and other relevant course operation details. Structured courses must have at least three evaluation components including a comprehensive exam at the end of the semester. Evaluation should be spread across. the semester.

First degree courses require at least 20 percent open-book written tests or take-home assignments, while higher degree courses require at least 40 percent open-book evaluation components. Students must attend all evaluations and submit work on time. If a student misses a quiz, test, exam, or assignment component, they must immediately contact the instructor-in-charge.

If the reason is genuine, the instructor may arrange a suitable make-up. The instructor-in-charge’s decision on make-up matters is final. Evaluation feedback should be continuous. Answer scripts should be evaluated promptly, shown to students for clarification, and returned where practical. Instructors should discuss exam performance with the class, including details like highest, lowest, and average. performance, so students understand their learning gaps and improve future academic work.

Letter grades carry grade points: A is Excellent with 10, A- is Very Good with 9, B is Good with 8, B- is Above Average with 7, C is Fair or Average with 6, C- is Below Average with 5, D is Poor. with 4, and E is Exposed with 2. Some courses use non-letter grades instead of grade points.

Seminar, independent study, teaching practice, internship, dissertation, project work, design project, capstone project, audit courses, and Ph.D. thesis may receive qualitative outcomes such as Excellent, Good, Fair, Poor, Satisfactory, Unsatisfactory, Acceptable, or Unacceptable depending on course type. Academic. reports I GA W RC RRA DP NC TGA AC. Reports are not grades but record academic situations. Incomplete means pending requirements.

Grade Awaited means grade delay. Withdrawn records approved withdrawal. Registration Cancelled, Required to Register Again, and Discontinued from Programme show registration consequences. Not Cleared indicates insufficient evaluable participation. Thesis Grade Awaited and Allowed. to Continue apply to specific thesis or dissertation situations. If a student has not completed some course requirement, the instructor may award an Incomplete report and inform the student.

The student must contact the instructor and complete requirements within the allowed time. If the Incomplete extends beyond next registration, the student may not register and. must seek permission to stay away. Grade Awaited may be used when grades are delayed due to unfair means investigation, indiscipline case, off-campus coordination, Practice School, WILP, or Ph.D. operational reasons.

The concerned Dean authorises GA and estimates when grades will be received. Further registration for students with GA is handled by the concerned Dean. Withdrawal W and Registration Cancelled RC meaning. A W report is recorded when course withdrawal is approved and is ignored for operations dependent on performance by looking back to previous valid performance.

RC means registration was cancelled. If the student must repeat the course it is reported as RRA, and if cancellation means programme discontinuation. it is reported as DP. NC is awarded when a registered student gives inadequate opportunity for evaluation by missing components, appearing only formally, submitting blank work, or not cooperating with academic tasks.

In compulsory courses, NC requires registration again for a valid grade. In electives, the student may repeat or choose another elective subject to. requirements. CGPA is the weighted average of grade points in all letter-graded courses from entry to the latest semester. Course units act as weights.

If a student repeats a course and earns a new grade, the new grade replaces the earlier grade in CGPA calculation. Reports alone do not change CGPA. Grade sheets may be. withheld if dues are unpaid or a discipline or unfair means case is pending.

Instructors must maintain attendance, evaluation records, class averages, mid-semester grading, question papers, and handouts for at least two semesters after course completion. These records support scrutiny, review, and academic monitoring. At the end of each semester, first degree students should not. have more than one E grade and must maintain CGPA at least 4.

Higher degree students should not have any E grade and must maintain CGPA at least 5. Students must also maintain required progress in clearing prescribed courses. Failure to meet minimum academic requirements is called an affliction and brings the student under Academic. Counselling Board supervision. While under ACB, the student loses normal registration options like course choice, electives, repetition, higher level courses, substitution, and withdrawal.

ACB decides the course package and monitors rehabilitation. ACB estimates deficiencies, sets semester-wise tasks, may require summer registration, and checks progress. If a student does not meet targets or remains unresponsive, ACB. can require discontinuation from the Institute. It may also recommend transfer to a less demanding programme where allowed. ACB decisions are final.

Students may receive a Practice School Transcript showing professional personality assessment. PS I normally requires prior preparation from earlier semesters.

PS II requires all named courses including PS I to be cleared. Students cannot register in any other course while taking PS. Unsatisfactory progress or unprofessional conduct in PS can lead to discontinuation from the PS programme option, with possible transfer to thesis or. dissertation route. Higher degree dissertation usually follows coursework.

Topic and supervisor are assigned through DCA with Dean approval. The final thesis or dissertation must be an English coherent document with supervisor certification and submitted within prescribed time. A first degree student may choose a 16-unit thesis with no concurrent courses, or a 9-unit thesis that may require extra electives to meet graduation requirements.

A 9-unit thesis student may register in at most three simultaneous courses totaling at most nine units, normally counted under open electives. If thesis or dissertation is not submitted. within time, the last registration may be cancelled and RRA reported. NC in thesis, seminar, or dissertation requires registration again for the same number of units or as decided by authority. BITS provides academic flexibilities such as admissions in both semesters,. advanced standing, marginal deficiency, transfer, dual degree, minor programmes, thesis or Practice School option, electives, course repetition, withdrawal, substitution, audit, summer term, and pace variation.

These flexibilities are conditional, competitive where applicable, and must not dilute academic standards. Admission may happen in both semesters depending on seats and facilities. In first tier programmes, most admissions. are in the first semester. Second semester admission may be possible for a small number of equally competitive students due to structural flexibility, but there is no guarantee that such admissions will always be offered.

Summer course offerings are voluntary and limited. During summer term, there is no prescribed semester course concept, but backlog remains. first priority. Students may register for at most three courses if rules permit. Higher level summer courses are allowed only when backlog conditions and prerequisite requirements are satisfied. Dual degree is a competitive opportunity for students capable of working toward two integrated first degrees.

The single degree semester pattern is replaced by a composite programme. Requirements include general institutional courses, discipline requirements of both degrees, electives, and PS or thesis components. The two degrees are completed together, not separately. A dual degree student cannot drop the first degree and continue only with the second degree. Withdrawal from dual degree requires written application and approval.

Willful departure from the approved dual. degree scheme may cancel the dual degree facility and transfer the student back to the first degree under current structure and regulations. Transfer means movement from one programme, stream, scheme, or tier goal to another before completing the first. At transfer, the full academic record is scrutinised.

Cleared courses may be booked into the new. programme where suitable, residual courses are handled by rules, and CGPA plus academic sanctions are carried forward. A candidate with preparation higher than the minimum entrance qualification may be considered for admission with advanced standing. Previous academic record is compared with programme requirements, and tests may be used if needed.

Exemption is usually only for. compulsory courses, while electives of the new programme generally must be completed at BITS. A strong candidate with small gaps in prior preparation may be admitted with marginal deficiency. The student may be required to complete extra courses beyond the normal programme.

Such students enter with a form of backlog, and CGPA or division is. calculated using both prescribed programme courses and required additional courses. Audit courses are mainly for knowledge updating, especially for casual students not pursuing a degree. Audit completion does not clear a course for degree requirements and cannot automatically be converted into credit later.

Practice School, thesis, dissertation, collaborative programme courses, and other restricted courses are. not normally available on audit. Ph.D. admission is initially provisional and becomes formal after passing the qualifying examination. DRC recommends a notional supervisor or mentor and appoints a Doctoral Advisory Committee.

The student must complete coursework, qualifying exam, language requirement, teaching practice or research methodology, seminar, thesis work, and other prescribed components on time. Ph.D. students with. first degree background may be prescribed at least six courses with at least 24 units. They must not get below D grade in any course and must maintain minimum CGPA 5.

Students with higher degree background may have zero to six courses depending on research area and preparation. The exam normally has written and oral parts and tests fundamentals, knowledge, and ability to handle unknown situations.

A student who fails or misses it may get only one more attempt at DRC discretion. A Ph.D. student may need to demonstrate ability to translate material from. a modern European language into English, unless waived. Thesis registration is allowed only after topic and supervisor approval. Final Ph.D. thesis should normally be submitted within ten semesters after qualifying examination. Submission requires thesis copies, soft copy, synopsis, supervisor certificate of original work, DAC reports, examiner list, and fee receipt. Ph.D. thesis is examined by two Vice-Chancellor-appointed examiners. Verdicts may approve viva, request minor or major corrections, require revision and resubmission, or reject the thesis. Disagreements. may involve discussion or third examiner. If approved, viva-voce is held, possibly online in extraordinary situations, and final thesis grade is awarded.

A Ph.D. student may not continue if qualifying exam is not passed in time, CGPA remains below 5.50 beyond allowed recovery, two consecutive thesis interim grades are unsatisfactory, thesis or revised thesis is. not submitted in time, thesis is rejected, final verdict is not obtained, or final thesis grade is unacceptable.

A first degree or higher degree student graduates after clearing all prescribed courses, completing thesis, seminar, dissertation, or unit requirements where applicable, meeting minimum CGPA of 4.50 for first degree or B.Sc. and 5.50 for higher degree,. remaining outside ACB, resolving NC consequences, and satisfying all academic regulations.

A student becomes eligible for the degree only after satisfying graduation requirements, evaluation rules, and having no pending indiscipline or unfair means case. If the student has outstanding dues to the Institute, hostel, or recognised organ of the Institute, the degree may be withheld. until those dues are fully cleared.

Integrated first degree and B.Sc. working professional programmes use CGPA-based division classification. Distinction requires CGPA 9.00 or more. First Division requires CGPA 7.00 or more but less than 9. Second Division requires CGPA 4.50 or more but less than 7. Diploma, higher degree, and Ph.D. programmes do not receive. divisions.
